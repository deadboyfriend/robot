Electrical Architecture

Power System

Ryobi ONE+ 18V tool batteries provide hot-swappable unlimited runtime. A single buck converter steps voltage down for the Pi. The Cytron MDD10A handles motor power natively at battery voltage.

Architecture Overview
Critical voltage note: Ryobi ONE+ batteries are 18V nominal but reach ~21V when fully charged (5S Li-ion: 5 × 4.2V). All motor-side components must be rated above 21V. The Cytron MDD10A (rated 30V) is chosen specifically for this. The TB6612FNG (max 15V) must NEVER be connected directly to the Ryobi battery — it is used only for secondary motors in Phase 3 running from a stepped-down supply.

Why Ryobi ONE+?

  • Multiple batteries already owned — zero cost
  • Hot-swappable: robot never needs to wait to recharge
  • Built-in BMS handles all safety (overcurrent, temperature, short circuit)
  • High capacity (even 2Ah = hours of pet-speed wandering)
  • Chargers already owned
  • Dock modification enables autonomous recharging (Phase 2+)
Polarity warning: The Cytron MDD10A has no reverse polarity protection on the motor power input (VMOT). Connecting the Ryobi adapter the wrong way will instantly destroy the board. Always verify polarity with a multimeter before connecting. Check: red wire = positive, black = negative. Measure at the adapter terminals before connecting to MDD10A.
Cytron MDD10A — Motor Driver

Why MDD10A not TB6612FNG?

The TB6612FNG has a 15V maximum motor voltage. The Ryobi battery peaks at 21V fully charged — this would immediately destroy the TB6612FNG. The MDD10A is rated to 30V, giving 9V of headroom over the battery's maximum. It also provides 10A continuous (vs 1.2A), which is far better suited to the SR8855's original drive motors.

Buck Converter — Pi Power

Specification requirements

The buck converter steps the Ryobi battery voltage down to 5V for the Pi. The input must accept up to 21V (battery fully charged). Order a "24V to 5V" variant — not "12V to 5V" — to ensure adequate headroom. Output must supply at least 3A for Pi 4 under load (it draws up to 3A peak).

Autonomous Dock Charging (Phase 2+)

The concept

The SR8855 docking station is modified to house a disassembled Ryobi ONE+ compact charger PCB. The dock's mains cable connects to the Ryobi charger's AC input. The charger's battery output connects to upgraded dock contact pins. When the robot docks (guided by IR homing), spring-loaded contacts connect the charger to the Ryobi battery mounted on the robot chassis.

This approach uses the genuine Ryobi charger IC — no custom charging circuit required. All temperature monitoring, cell balancing, and BMS communication is handled by the Ryobi charger hardware exactly as designed.

Contact pin upgrade required

The original SR8855 dock has 2 contact pins (V+, V−). Ryobi charging requires at minimum 3 pins: V+, V−, and T (thermistor for temperature monitoring). The dock contact mechanism must be upgraded to a 3–4 pin arrangement. New spring contacts can be sourced cheaply and fitted alongside the originals.

Routing challenge

The Ryobi battery sits on top of the robot. Dock contacts are on the bottom. Wires must route from the battery adapter (top) through the chassis to the contact pads (bottom). Plan this routing during the initial teardown — leave a cable path clear before reassembling.

Recommended charger: Ryobi PCG001 or RC18120 (compact charger). Second-hand from eBay ~£10–15. Compact PCB size makes fitting inside the dock realistic. Slow-ish charge rate is fine — the robot isn't in a hurry and can charge overnight.
Battery Mounting on Chassis

Option A — 3D printed dock

3D printed Ryobi tool body rail mounted to chassis top. Battery plugs in from above. Thingiverse has Ryobi ONE+ dock models. Looks intentional — "energy pack" aesthetic suits the robot pet character. Best option if 3D printer access available.

Option B — L-bracket

Aluminium right-angle extrusion from B&Q (~£3) bolted through chassis lid. Battery adapter clamped to bracket. No printer needed, available today. Less elegant but fully functional.

Option C — Dust bin cavity

Former dust bin compartment (~120×80×70mm). Compact 2Ah Ryobi may fit. Keeps low profile. Measure before committing — tight fit only works with slimmest battery variants.
