Architecture Decision Records

Decision Log

Key architectural decisions with context, alternatives considered, and rationale. This page preserves the thinking behind the build for future reference — including for AI assistants picking up this project.

For AI assistants reading this: These decisions are settled. Don't suggest reverting them unless there is a compelling technical reason specific to a new phase requirement. Each decision was made after considering alternatives — the context is captured below.
Decisions
ADR-001
Replace Samsung PCB entirely rather than piggybacking
Accepted
Context

The Samsung SR8855 has a proprietary main PCB that controls motors, sensors, and navigation. Two options existed: (A) reverse-engineer/intercept the Samsung board's signals, or (B) remove it entirely and wire all motors/sensors directly to the Pi.

Decision

Option B — full replacement. Remove the Samsung PCB. Wire drive motors to Cytron MDD10A. Wire sensors directly to Pi GPIO. Samsung firmware and software not used at all.

Rationale

The SR8855's proprietary protocol is undocumented. Option A would likely have been a dead end, consuming significant time with no guarantee of success. Option B is well-documented, has huge community support, and gives complete control. The Samsung PCB provides no value once the robot's purpose changes from vacuum to pet.

ADR-002
Cytron MDD10A as primary motor driver (not TB6612FNG)
Accepted
Context

Originally the TB6612FNG was selected as the motor driver. After the decision to use Ryobi ONE+ batteries (ADR-003), a voltage compatibility issue was identified: TB6612FNG maximum VM is 15V, but Ryobi ONE+ peaks at 21V fully charged. A replacement was needed.

Decision

Cytron MDD10A. Rated 5–30V, 10A continuous per channel. Handles Ryobi's 21V peak with 9V headroom. Pi-compatible 3.3V logic.

Alternatives considered

L298N: Handles 46V but bulky, inefficient, runs hot — rejected. BTS7960: 27V rated, 43A — overkill, larger footprint — rejected. Step-down + TB6612FNG: Add a 12V buck converter before the TB6612FNG — adds complexity, another failure point — rejected in favour of a single capable driver. MDD3A: Cytron's smaller sibling — only 16V max, also incompatible with Ryobi — rejected.

Note on TB6612FNG

The TB6612FNG already ordered (AliExpress) is retained for Phase 3 secondary motors (sweeper motors, vacuum motor) which will run from a stepped-down 12V supply — well within the 15V limit.

ADR-003
Ryobi ONE+ 18V batteries as primary power source
Accepted
Context

The SR8855's original 14.4V Li-ion battery was the obvious starting point. The question was whether to reuse it or replace it. Simon already owns multiple Ryobi ONE+ 18V batteries from a full tool collection.

Decision

Ryobi ONE+ 18V batteries. Hot-swappable, already owned, built-in BMS, large capacity range (2–9Ah), and existing chargers already owned.

Rationale

The original Samsung battery is 14.4V (4S Li-ion), non-standard, and eventually needs replacing. Ryobi ONE+ batteries: (1) are already owned at zero additional cost, (2) are hot-swappable for unlimited runtime — a key character feature, (3) have a mature BMS handling all safety, (4) enable autonomous recharging via a modified dock (ADR-007). The 18V nominal voltage required changing the motor driver selection (ADR-002), which was an acceptable trade-off.

ADR-004
Breadboard-first approach — no soldering in Phase 1
Accepted
Decision

All Phase 1 electronics are built on a solderless breadboard. Test motor and AA batteries used for bench validation. Only after the complete circuit is verified does it get integrated into the chassis.

Rationale

Simon is new to electronics. Soldering errors on a permanent board are hard to debug and potentially irreversible. A breadboard approach: (1) is fully reversible, (2) makes troubleshooting easier — components can be moved or replaced instantly, (3) allows the circuit to be validated with cheap test components before connecting to the actual vacuum motors and Ryobi battery. Phase 2+ can introduce soldered connections as confidence grows.

ADR-005
Raspberry Pi 4 (8GB) retained — no SBC swap
Accepted
Context

Radxa Rock 5C was considered as an alternative SBC (stronger NPU for AI vision). The Rock 5C uses 1.8V GPIO vs Pi's 3.3V.

Decision

Keep Raspberry Pi 4 (8GB) already owned. Rock 5C not pursued for Phase 1 or 2.

Rationale

The GPIO voltage difference (1.8V vs 3.3V) would require level shifters on every component — significant added complexity for Phase 1. The Pi 4 ecosystem is unmatched for tutorials, community support, and library availability. For a first electronics build, this matters more than raw AI performance. The Pi 4 8GB is capable of YOLOv8-nano at 5–8fps (sufficient for Phase 2 navigation decisions) and Phase 4's LLM runs on a home PC regardless. Rock 5C can be revisited if Phase 4 vision requirements exceed Pi 4 capability.

ADR-006
Reuse SR8855 ceiling camera for VSLAM navigation
Accepted
Context

The SR8855 has a top-mounted camera for ceiling-referenced visual navigation. During teardown this was confirmed as a real camera (not IR/TOF). Connector type TBD.

Decision

Retain and reuse the ceiling camera for VSLAM. Investigate connector during Phase 1 teardown. If compatible with Pi CSI — connect directly. If not — mount a Pi Camera Module 3 in the same position pointing up.

Rationale

The ceiling is the best navigation surface — static, well-lit, full of consistent landmarks (light fittings, ceiling roses). A ceiling-facing camera enables room recognition (different rooms have different ceiling patterns), position tracking, and purposeful navigation — not just random wandering. A robot with spatial memory feels categorically more alive than one that bounces randomly. This is the single most impactful sensor reuse from the original chassis.

ADR-007
Autonomous dock charging via Ryobi charger PCB inside SR8855 dock
Accepted
Decision

A disassembled Ryobi compact charger (PCG001 or RC18120) is fitted inside the SR8855 dock housing. The dock's mains cable connects to the charger PCB. Charger output connects to upgraded dock contacts (3–4 pins). Robot docks via IR homing, charging completes autonomously.

Alternatives considered

Reuse original dock charging circuit: Original circuit designed for 14.4V 4S pack. Voltage/protocol mismatch with Ryobi 5S pack — rejected as unsafe. Custom CC/CV circuit: Build a bespoke charger — complex, risk of Li-ion mismanagement — rejected. Manual battery swap only: Robot alerts family when low, humans swap — simpler but misses the autonomous pet character of self-care.

Rationale

Using the genuine Ryobi charger PCB eliminates all Li-ion safety concerns. The charger handles temperature monitoring, cell balancing, and BMS communication exactly as designed. The engineering problem reduces to mechanical fitting and contact routing — well within scope. The dock's IR homing emitters are preserved and reused. Planned for Phase 2+.

ADR-008
LLM runs on home PC via Ollama — not on Pi
Accepted
Decision

Ollama runs on a capable home PC on the home WiFi network. The Pi 4 calls it over HTTP. Models: Phi-3 Mini (3.8B) or Llama 3.2 3B. Pi handles audio I/O only.

Rationale

The Pi 4 runs any useful LLM unacceptably slowly (multi-minute response times). A home PC generates responses in under 2 seconds — natural for a robot pet. Ollama is free, easy to set up, and the WiFi call adds negligible latency on a home network. The Pi's resources are reserved for real-time tasks: vision, motor control, audio. This architecture also allows upgrading the LLM model at any time without touching the robot.

ADR-009
Sweeper motors repurposed as physical expression actuators
Accepted
Decision

The SR8855's two sweeper motors are repurposed as Phase 3 expression actuators — driving physical ears, antennae, or other appendages that convey mood through movement.

Rationale

Physical movement is categorically more powerful for anthropomorphisation than lights or sound alone. The sweeper motors are already gear-reduced (slow, low-torque — perfect for expressive movement), already in the chassis, and at zero cost. Compare WALL-E's eye movements or a dog's ears — physical expression is what makes observers emotionally bond with robots. This single reuse decision significantly raises the character ceiling of Phase 3 without adding cost or new components.
