Wiring Guide

Step-by-step wiring for Phase 1 bench testing. Start on breadboard with AA batteries and a test motor — validate the circuit before touching the vacuum chassis.

Before You Start
Breadboard-first rule: Build and test the entire circuit on a breadboard with a 4×AA battery pack and a small spare DC motor. Only when the Python test script drives the motor correctly should you progress to connecting the vacuum's motors and Ryobi battery. This prevents expensive mistakes on the real chassis.
Expert review — Critical safety requirement: The SR8855's OEM sensors (cliff, obstacle, bump, Hall encoders) were originally powered by the Samsung PCB which had 3.3V and 5V rails. Individual sensors may output 5V signals. Connecting a 5V signal to Pi 4 GPIO (3.3V max, NOT 5V tolerant) will destroy the GPIO bank instantly and silently. Verify every sensor's output voltage before connecting to the Pi. See the sensor verification table below.

Safety checklist

  • Verify all OEM sensor voltages before connecting to Pi GPIO
  • 470µF+ capacitor across MDD10A VMOT/GND before powering
  • Emergency stop button wired before any floor testing
  • Measure battery voltage before connecting anything (4×AA = ~6V, Ryobi = 18–21V)
  • Verify MDD10A polarity with multimeter — no reverse protection
  • Pi powered OFF before wiring GPIO connections
  • Motor battery direct to MDD10A terminals — NOT through breadboard rails
  • Double-check all connections against table below before powering on

Test with LED first

Replace the motor and battery with a single LED (any colour) and 330Ω resistor between AO1 and GND. When the Python test script runs, the LED flickers with PWM. This confirms all connections are correct without motor/battery risk. Once working — swap in the real motor.

SR8855 Sensor Voltage Verification — Do This First
Procedure for each sensor: Power the sensor from 3.3V (Pi pin 1). Trigger it (cover cliff sensor with hand, block obstacle sensor, press bump switch). Measure output pin with multimeter. If output exceeds 3.4V — add a voltage divider before connecting to Pi GPIO. Results must be documented in the table below before any GPIO connection is made.

Voltage divider formula: To drop 5V signal to 3.3V-safe: use 10kΩ resistor from sensor output to Pi GPIO pin, and 20kΩ resistor from Pi GPIO pin to GND. The Pi GPIO input then reads approximately 3.3V when sensor outputs 5V. This is safe and costs about 2p in resistors.
Critical Addition — VMOT Decoupling Capacitor
This must be fitted before powering the motor circuit for the first time. Without it, motor back-EMF spikes can instantly destroy the MDD10A. Identified as the single most likely cause of first-build hardware failure in the expert review.

Capacitor placement

Place a 470µF–1000µF electrolytic capacitor rated 35V+ with its leads connected across the VMOT (+) and GND (−) rails on the breadboard, as physically close to the MDD10A as possible. The capacitor's negative lead (marked with stripe) connects to GND. The positive lead connects to VMOT.

Emergency Stop Wiring

Physical E-Stop button

Wire a normally-closed (NC) momentary pushbutton in series between the motor battery positive terminal and the MDD10A VMOT input. When pressed, it breaks the motor supply circuit instantly — regardless of software state. If the Pi crashes, freezes, or enters an uncontrolled state, pressing this button stops all motor movement immediately.

Button typeNormally-Closed (NC) — circuit open when pressed PositionIn series with motor battery positive wire to MDD10A VMOT MountingAccessible on chassis exterior — top or side, reachable in emergency RatingMust be rated for 21V DC and at least 15A (motor stall current)

Sensor Wiring — SR8855 OEM Sensors
Assess before buying replacements: The SR8855's cliff sensors and obstacle sensors may be directly usable with the Pi. During teardown, photograph the sensor connector types and wire colours, then probe the output with a multimeter to determine: signal voltage (3.3V or 5V), output type (digital HIGH/LOW or analog), and number of wires (typically 3: VCC, GND, OUT). 3.3V digital output = plug straight into Pi GPIO. 5V output = needs a voltage divider or level shifter.
